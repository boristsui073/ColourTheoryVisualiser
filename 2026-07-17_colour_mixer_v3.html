Notes
#FF00FF represents the furthest "horizontal" point away from the central axis in the sRGB gamut volume across all lightness levels.
Therefore, it is used to represent the edge of the radial grid.
The edge of the radial grid indicates the maximum chroma a colour can be within the sRGB colour volume.
Every colour in this visualiser is an sRGB colour. sRGB is the standard colour space of consumer screens, and it defines a fixed volume of displayable colours — the gamut.
The shapes shown here — the layer boundaries, the wedge fills, the radial grid limit — are all properties of that volume. They mark where sRGB runs out, not where colour itself ends.
A display with a wider gamut (such as Display-P3) can show colours beyond these boundaries, and the shapes would extend accordingly.
The visualiser measures and arranges sRGB colours using OKLCh coordinates: lightness, chroma, and hue.
The way this visualiser arranges colour follows the system introduced by Albert Munsell in the early 1900s.
Munsell organised colour by three perceptual dimensions: hue arranged around a circle, value (lightness) as the vertical axis, and chroma as the distance outward from the central grey axis.
He eventually observed that the resulting solid is irregular — different hues reach their maximum chroma at different values, so the volume bulges and dips rather than forming a neat cylinder. That is exactly the shape seen here.
The visualiser keeps Munsell's arrangement but uses modern OKLCh coordinates for the actual numbers.
